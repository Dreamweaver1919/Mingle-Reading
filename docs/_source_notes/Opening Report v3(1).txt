Ⅱ. Rationale for the project
1. Research Background and Motivation
With the rapid development of large language models and intelligent agent technologies, digital reading platforms have the potential to evolve from static text display into dynamic, dialogue‑driven intellectual experiences. However, most current e‑reading applications still treat reading as a solitary activity. Readers struggle with complex character relationships, unfamiliar historical backgrounds, and a lack of timely interpretation, which often leads to low completion rates and shallow understanding. According to a recent survey of college student readers, nearly 96% percent of respondents admitted giving up on classic literary works because they had no one to explain difficult passages or to summarize chapters from a knowledgeable perspective. Figure 1 illustrates the initial motivation and pre‑investigation of our project. The findings indicate that readers urgently desire a form of guidance that is not merely a dictionary or a generic AI summary, but rather a personified, opinionated companion who can walk through the text with them.
Figure 1.Survey on Readers' Deep Reading Pain Points
The lack of such guidance has direct consequences on reading retention and intellectual engagement. Data from major digital reading platforms show that the average time a user spends on a single e‑book is less than three and a half hours, and the completion rate for full‑length books remains below fifteen percent. For titles with complex narratives or rich historical contexts, the abandonment rate often exceeds eighty percent. Meanwhile, the transition to digital reading has historically resulted in one-way information delivery that lacks interactivity. Current consumer trends favor "instant gratification" media, such as short-form videos, which contribute to the decline of deep reading and systematic cultural engagement. Survey data indicates that 50% of students struggle to comprehend deep textual connotations, and 58.33% face difficulties in maintaining focus during long-form reading. Readers report feeling lost when they encounter unfamiliar references, and they miss the kind of spontaneous, insightful commentary that a human teacher or a well‑read friend might provide during a shared reading session.
Figure 2 presents a detailed survey on what readers want most during digital reading. The top one demand is Guiding Deep Thinking (35%). Notably, many readers also expressed interest in a proactive feature where the AI guide itself highlights noteworthy passages and offers interpretations without being asked.

Meanwile, Academic research has demonstrated the effectiveness of role‑based AI in improving reading comprehension. For instance, a study from Stanford University showed that students who discussed literary texts with a character‑driven conversational agent performed significantly better on post‑reading assessments. Based on these observations, this project responds to the growing demand for deeper, more social forms of digital reading by proposing a character‑guided, LLM‑agent‑based system named Muse Reading. The aim is to reconstruct the relationship between readers and books through the dual paths of persona construction and real‑time interactive guidance. The system focuses on readers’ real pain points, such as the lack of contextual interpretation and post‑chapter synthesis, and provides a virtual mentor who not only answers questions but also initiates discussions, highlights meaningful segments, and summarizes each chapter from a consistent, character‑specific perspective.

Research on domestic and international development status
Domestic Development
Efforts to enhance digital reading through artificial intelligence have emerged both in China and abroad. These efforts can be broadly categorized into three directions: automated summarization, voice assistants, and conversational AI agents.
In China, WeChat Reading, the largest digital reading platform, offers an AI narration feature with multiple voice options and basic chapter summaries. The Dedao app provides a reading assistant that generates mind maps and collects key quotes from a book. However, none of these functions involve role‑playing or character‑guided interaction. Domestic platforms such as China Literature (Yuewen) and Zhangyue have integrated "IP+AI" features, primarily utilizing Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). However, these implementations are largely limited to "knowledge base Q&A," acting as static information administrators. They generally lack consistent character personas and suffer from "omniscient perspective" issues, where AI inadvertently provides spoilers, disrupting the narrative flow.
Figure 3. Shortcomings of the widely-used reading app WeChat Reading
In accordance with the Opinions on Promoting and Implementing the National Cultural Digitalization Strategy issued by the General Office of the Communist Party of China Central Committee and the General Office of the State Council, the state explicitly proposes to "develop new scenarios for digital cultural consumption". By constructing digital reading companions based on AI agents, this project actively responds to the digital transformation of the traditional publishing industry. It aims to leverage cutting-edge interactive technologies to promote nationwide reading and enhance China’s cultural soft power.
In 2026, the Cyberspace Administration of China and the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology pointed out in the Guiding Opinions on Accelerating the Engineering Application of General Artificial Intelligence that efforts shall be made to encourage the development of industry-level intelligent agents featuring strong cognition, in-depth interaction and high adaptability. The character Skills System developed in this project transforms large language models from a simple "dialogue tool" into an industry-specific Agent equipped with professional knowledge reserve and narrative logical capabilities. It is fully aligned with the national technological orientation that drives generative artificial intelligence to shift from "empowering daily life" to "in-depth scenario-based application".
AI has no fixed persona, no personal historical knowledge, and no ability to adopt the tone or viewpoint of a specific author or literary figure. Consequently, the reading experience remains largely solitary and instrumental. Only an anthropomorphic reading companion, rather than encyclopedia-style rigid annotations, can create an immersive reading atmosphere. It maintains the integrity of the reader’s train of thought without interrupting their reading rhythm and cognitive continuity. Moreover, no existing product in China supports proactive highlighting of paragraphs by the AI guide or summarization that consistently reflects a chosen persona’s unique perspective.
International Development
Outside China, generative AI has enabled several reading‑related innovations. Amazon Kindle has recently piloted an “AI Book Review” feature that generates automated chapter summaries, but the summaries are neutral in tone and lack any character identity. Google NotebookLM can convert uploaded documents into a podcast‑style dialogue between two speakers, yet it is not designed for real‑time, in‑page interaction while reading.
Third‑party plugins built on ChatGPT, such as “ScholarAI,” can explain academic paragraphs but use a generic, scholarly voice rather than a specific historical role. Character.AI offers numerous virtual personas, including Socrates and Shakespeare, but these characters cannot be bound to the specific book the user is reading, nor can they automatically sense the current highlighted text and produce a contextual response without manual copy‑and‑paste. This breaks the immersive flow.
For the character building module, we researched on some of the most popular international AI agents website:
· Persona Modeling: Character.ai and Inworld AI lead in multimodal personality engines. Inworld AI, in particular, utilizes emotional dynamics modules and long-term memory to maintain persona consistency with a latency of less than 200ms.
· Decision Simulation: Academic research, such as the "Character is Destiny" study (2024), has validated the use of the CharMap method to allow LLMs to simulate character-specific decision-making based on original text backgrounds.
· Knowledge Management: The MEGa (Memory Embedded in Gated LLMs) framework demonstrates a superior method for knowledge injection via gated LoRA modules, offering a technical paradigm for long-term persona consistency and narrative-aware responses.
For agent memory building module, the followings are the example methods found in recent research works:
ReadAgent (ICML 2024, Google DeepMind) employs gist memory for long-text QA efficiency, achieving 3-20x context expansion. However, its goal is post-hoc QA after full-book reading; all gists remain open for retrieval with no visibility constraints.
CAM (2025) introduces a constructivist three-layer graph memory for reading comprehension. While structurally closest to PBM, it partitions text into fixed 512-token blocks, lacks visibility control and emotional capability, and performs cluster fusion (merging similar content) rather than preserving original understanding alongside new insights.HippoRAG (NeurIPS 2024) demonstrates strong entity-relation graph retrieval but targets open-domain QA without progressive reading constraints.
In emotion-aware memory: MemoryBank (AAAI 2024) introduced Ebbinghaus forgetting curves; Livia (AIVR 2025) implemented emotion-salience-driven selective forgetting; REMT (Frontiers in AI, 2026) proposed emotional topology with a Mood Index. None of these systems have been validated in reading scenarios, nor do they include visibility control.
Taken together, no existing reading system simultaneously achieves all of the following: persona construction based on LLM agents, real‑time interaction over user‑highlighted text, proactive paragraph highlighting by the AI guide, and chapter‑end summarization delivered in the voice of a chosen character. Muse Reading is designed to fill this gap and to open a new reading paradigm comparable to how “vibe coding” has redefined programming.
3. Project objectives, content, expected results
3.1 Project Objectives
The core objective of our project is to develop an innovative solution that addresses critical gaps in the current digital reading experience. Traditional reading applications primarily focus on the one-way delivery and static management of content, lacking depth, interactivity, and intellectual companionship. Specifically, we identify the following key challenges:
(1) A Solitary and Non-Immersive Process: Current digital reading remains an isolated dialogue between the reader and static text, devoid of the companionship of a mentor or peer, which hinders emotional connection and immersive engagement.
(2) Weak and Static Comprehension Support: Existing features like footnotes or hyperlinked encyclopedias are predefined and static. They cannot provide dynamic, conversational, and deep explanations based on the reader's real-time questions, personal knowledge background, or reading progress.
(3) Lack of Profound Synthesis and Perspective Expansion: Upon finishing a chapter or book, there is typically a lack of in-depth summarization and perspective exchange. Readers struggle to systematize their takeaways and rarely have the opportunity to encounter valuable, alternative viewpoints different from the author's or the text's own.
(4) Difficulty in Scaling High-Quality, Personalized Guidance: One-on-one expert companionship is cost-prohibitive and cannot be scaled. There is currently no mature technological solution that can provide a scalable "reading mentor" experience characterized by a specific persona, erudition, and a unique intellectual style.
To tackle these issues, we aim to leverage the capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs), combined with Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and sophisticated prompt engineering, to construct a system of "Lead Reader Agents." Through multimodal interaction design, we seek to enhance reading depth and enjoyment while ensuring smooth and natural intellectual exchange. Our specific expectations can be outlined in three main aspects:
(1) Reconstruct a Deeply Immersive Reading Experience:
Develop a dynamic companion-reading system powered by "Lead Reader Agents." By allowing readers to choose a Lead Reader "portrayed" by a historical figure, author, or character from the book, we transform solitary reading into a cross-temporal dialogue. The Lead Reader, using their distinctive tone, knowledge base, and values, will guide the reading pace, provide contextual narration, insights, and pose questions at pivotal moments, creating an immersive atmosphere of "reading alongside a wise mind" and fundamentally alleviating the sense of solitude.
(2) Provide Dynamic, Context-Aware Interpretation and Cognitive Support:
Design real-time, non-intrusive interaction mechanisms that enable readers to engage with both the text and the Lead Reader at any moment. This includes: readers actively highlighting text for personalized Q&A; the Lead Reader proactively annotating and interpreting key passages, presenting insights via "comment bubbles"; and at the end of each chapter, the Lead Reader facilitating synthesis and perspective discussion. This process aims to activate static text into a dynamic field of knowledge and ideas, deepening comprehension and stimulating critical thinking.
(3) Personalized Intellectual Companionship:
Through technological innovation, translate the "masterclass" level of reading guidance—once available only to a few—into a personalized service accessible at scale. The system will record each reader's preferences, interaction history, and comprehension level, allowing the Lead Reader's interactions and explanations to adapt accordingly. Our goal is to enable every reader, regardless of their starting point, to have access to an exclusive, high-quality reading companion and intellectual mentor at an affordable cost, thereby democratizing the value and experience of deep reading.

3.2 Project Contents
3.2.1 Main Workflow: From User Interaction to Reading Experience Output
The project content of MUSE Reading follows an end-to-end workflow centered on agent-mediated reading. Its overall structure is depicted in Figure X. The reader first interacts with the front-end web reader by uploading or opening a text, selecting passages, entering questions, and creating notes or highlights. These user actions are passed to the agent-centric reading core, where the multi-agent orchestrator determines whether the request should be handled by a literary master imitation agent, a book character agent, the intelligent Bubble engine, or the note-taking and research support modules. The agent core then calls the knowledge retrieval and memory layer to obtain relevant text snippets, background knowledge, author materials, character information, and the reader's current reading position. Finally, the system generates outputs through three primary channels: chat sidebar responses, intelligent Bubble annotations, and notes or research-oriented outputs. In this workflow, the front end is responsible for interaction and display, the agent core is responsible for orchestration and generation, and the retrieval and understanding layer provides grounded knowledge support.
Figure 5. Project Overall Architecture
3.2.2 Front-End Interaction Layer
The Front-End Interaction Layer, depicted in Figure x, defines the user-facing entry points through which readers interact with the Muse Reading system. The structure begins with three basic user actions: opening a book, selecting text, and asking questions or saving reading materials. These actions are captured by the Web Reader Interface, which organizes the reading area, selected passages, navigation, and reading progress into a unified interaction environment.
The key function of this layer is not to perform reasoning itself, but to collect user intent and deliver system responses in a readable form. Once a reader selects a passage or submits a question, the interface converts this action into an agent request. The response generated by the back-end agent system then returns to the reader through three major interaction channels: the Chat Sidebar, Intelligent Bubble, and Notes & Highlights. The Chat Sidebar supports direct Q&A and guided dialogue; the Intelligent Bubble provides inline contextual tips and annotations; and the Notes & Highlights module allows readers to preserve useful interpretations as part of their reading record.
Personalization is also connected to this layer through reading preferences and reading history. This means that the front end not only displays content, but also records how the reader interacts with the system. Overall, this layer serves as the bridge between the reader and the intelligent back-end modules, ensuring that reading, questioning, annotation, and note-taking remain integrated within the same reading interface.
Figure 6. Front-End Interaction Layer Structure
3.2.3 Agent-Centric AI Reading Core
As shown in Figure xx, the Agent Core Layer is the central coordination layer of the Muse Reading system. Its main role is to receive user queries or selected passages from the front-end interface, determine which agent or module should respond, and organize the final response into an appropriate interaction format. As shown in the structure, the Multi-Agent Orchestrator is placed at the center of this layer because it is responsible for routing, coordinating, and triggering different agent behaviors.
A key technical component in this layer is RAG + Prompt Engineering. The agent first retrieves relevant contextual information from the knowledge base, such as text snippets, background materials, character-related information, and reading-position metadata. The retrieved content is then combined with carefully designed prompts to guide the model’s generation. In this way, RAG strengthens the generated text by making it more grounded, context-aware, and consistent with the selected reading task.
The Memory Manager works together with the orchestrator and the RAG-based generation process. It maintains dialogue memory, character consistency, reading position, and anti-spoiler control. These memory-related constraints are especially important for literary reading, because a character agent should respond only according to the reader’s current progress and should not reveal future plot information. Therefore, the memory mechanism is not separated from generation; rather, it provides boundaries for what information can be retrieved and used during response generation.
The lower part of the diagram shows the two major agent types supported by the core layer. Literary Master Agents, such as Lu Xun, Mark Twain, and Eileen Chang, are designed for guided reading, interpretation, and dialogue from a distinctive authorial perspective. The Book Character Agent follows a workflow from source text to character extraction, role setup, boundary control, and in-character dialogue. This structure allows the system to support both master-style interpretation and immersive character interaction.
The process flow at the bottom further clarifies the responsibility of the Agent Core. A user query or selected text is first received by the Agent Orchestrator, then processed through RAG-enhanced prompting and memory control, routed to the appropriate master agent or character agent, and finally delivered through chat, bubble, or notes. Therefore, the Agent Core is not a single chatbot module, but an orchestration layer that connects retrieval, memory, role construction, and RAG-enhanced text generation.
Figure 7. Reading Agent Core Structure
3.2.4 Knowledge Retrieval and Reading Understanding Layer
The Knowledge Retrieval & Understanding Layer's structure is listed in Figure xxx. This component provides the supporting knowledge and comprehension capabilities required by the Agent Core. It does not directly replace the agent layer; instead, it supplies the contextual materials and reading-related information that the orchestrator may invoke when needed. The structure is organized into four functional parts: content ingestion, knowledge base, reading understanding, and supporting modules.
Content ingestion is the first stage of this layer. It handles uploaded materials by performing basic processing steps such as upload, segmentation, and extraction. These steps prepare the original reading materials for later retrieval and understanding. After ingestion, the processed content enters the knowledge base, which stores text snippets, background information, and related resources. This knowledge base provides the factual and contextual foundation for later explanations, annotations, and discussions.
The Reading Understanding module focuses on how the system interprets the reader’s current reading context. It supports highlighting, explanation, and discussion, which correspond to common reading scenarios where users need help understanding a selected passage or reflecting on a broader topic. These capabilities provide intermediate comprehension support before the final response is generated and presented to the user.
The Supporting Modules section groups together several on-demand capabilities, including Intelligent Bubble, Note-Taking, and Personalization & Research. These modules are not independent technical layers, but auxiliary capabilities that support different output needs. Intelligent Bubble contributes commentary, concept explanation, and contextual information; Note-Taking organizes notes, highlights, and bookmarks; and Personalization & Research supports recommendation, citation, and outline-related services.
The bottom process flow summarizes the function of this layer: reader content is processed into usable knowledge, converted into contextual supply, and then used to support bubble annotations, notes, and research-related outputs. In this sense, the Knowledge Retrieval & Understanding Layer acts as the system’s knowledge infrastructure, while the Agent Core decides when and how these capabilities should be used.

4. Innovative points and project features
4.1 Innovative Points from the Reading Experience Perspective
(1) An author’s mind as your reading companion, not a silent dictionary
Traditional reading tools offer neutral, decontextualized definitions. Our core experiential innovation is to make the annotation come alive with the distinctive intellectual character of a chosen master writer. If the reader selects the perspective of Lu Xun, every annotation is filtered through his cold, penetrating dissection of social pathology—not merely explaining a phrase, but diagnosing the human weakness it exposes as Lu Xun himself might have done. If the reader switches to Oscar Wilde, the same passage is met with paradoxical inversions and an aesthetic critique that reverses conventional morality. This transforms the act of seeking help from a one-directional query into a stylized, opinionated dialogue. The reader no longer deciphers the text alone but walks through it accompanied by a formidable living mind, turning information retrieval into a meeting of perspectives. The value lies in replacing shallow comprehension with active intellectual friction: the reader is provoked to agree or disagree, which is the essence of deep literary engagement.
(2) Characters who whisper their unspoken thoughts, bringing the subtext to the surface
Existing reading companions remain strictly passive; they only speak when the reader explicitly asks. We introduce a proactive dimension of experience: at precisely those moments when the narrative tension peaks or a character’s hidden motivation is most palpable, the system allows the fictional character to initiate a brief, first-person Bubble annotation expressing inner thoughts the original text leaves unsaid. For instance, when the reader reaches the scene where a protagonist makes a morally ambiguous choice, the character might spontaneously murmur a line of self-justification or secret regret that matches the psychological profile established in the book. This is designed not to interrupt but to resonate, appearing gently at the edge of the reading flow. The innovation turns solitary reading into a form of overhearing the character’s conscience, dramatically deepening empathy and making the story’s emotional undercurrents tangible. It answers the longing many readers have to know what a character was really thinking beyond the printed words.
4.2 Innovative Points from the Technical Implementation Perspective
(1) A dual-layer anti-spoiler mechanism that hard-gates data and softly constrains behavior
This is our direct technical attack on the problem of the omniscient assistant that destroys suspense. The first layer is a deterministic data filter. At the moment the book text is embedded and stored, every chunk is tagged with precise positional metadata including its chapter index and section index. When anti-spoiler mode is active, the retrieval pipeline enforces a rigid pre-filter: any chunk whose chapter index is greater than that of the reader’s current position is entirely excluded from the candidate set before semantic similarity is even computed. No future plot information can possibly enter the model’s context window through the retrieval path. The second layer is a prompt-level cognitive boundary. The character agent’s system prompt is explicitly instructed that it knows only what the reader has already encountered and that it has no memory of narrative developments beyond the current chapter, which counteracts any pretraining memorization the large language model may have of this famous work. The synergy of a hard data gate and a soft behavioral guard creates the first truly robust progressive disclosure system for literary companionship, requiring no custom model components and leaving zero room for accidental spoilers.
(2) Lightweight persona skill distillation by transplanting an author’s analytical framework into a reasoning scaffold
Persona depth without fine-tuning is achieved not by surface-level mimicry alone but by capturing how a writer thinks. We perform a zero-training form of skill distillation: instead of just telling the model to sound like Lu Xun, we extract the logical sequence that characterizes his criticism—for example, “first isolate a mundane daily detail, then reveal the deep-rooted social illness it symptomizes, then connect it to a recurring historical pattern”—and encode this as a structured chain of reasoning within the system prompt. The large language model then follows this explicit thinking scaffold at inference time, producing output that reflects not just the author’s tone but their ingrained intellectual habits. The effect is that annotations carry genuine analytical bite, such as Wilde spotting the secret philistinism behind a sentimental phrase or Lu Xun uncovering the self-deception within a character’s virtue. This prompt-level transfer makes persona simulation accessible on consumer-grade hardware, circumventing the need for expensive fine-tuning while still achieving opinionated, expert-like responses that a generic assistant could never generate.
(3) A reading-position-aware trigger algorithm that senses narrative emotional density to activate character bubbles
To make character-initiated inner thoughts appear at the right instant without disrupting reading, we designed a lightweight orchestration algorithm. It monitors the reader’s current chapter and scroll position against a pre-annotated map of narrative emotional peaks: moments surrounding intense dialogue, the sudden appearance of a symbolic object, or the silent aftermath of a climactic event. When the reader pauses at one of these emotionally charged segments beyond a short threshold, the algorithm interprets this as a meaningful affective lingering. It then triggers the character agent to generate a single restrained first-person whisper that unveils a layer of subtext exactly where it is most contextually resonant. The design principle is strict restraint: the goal is not frequent pop-ups but rare, well-placed interventions that feel like the character suddenly confiding in the reader. This transforms the passive act of scrolling into a rhythmically attuned dialogue that respects both the story’s pacing and the reader’s immersion.
(4) A no-training architecture that reuses an open-source skeleton and relies entirely on prompt engineering plus retrieval-augmented generation
The entire system is built by extending the production-grade open-source reader SageRead, not by building from scratch. We inject three lightweight extensions: a floating Bubble annotation layer for both master and character voices, a custom retrieval backend that enforces the anti-spoiler filter, and a real-time stream of reading progress metadata into every inference request. All intelligent behaviors—authorial opinion generation, consistent character role-play, proactive inner monologue triggering—are implemented purely through prompt engineering and retrieval-augmented generation, with zero model fine-tuning. This means a student team equipped with only an eight-gigabyte consumer graphics card can deliver innovations that rival commercial platforms. The architectural value is extreme portability: to onboard a new master writer for any public-domain work, one simply replaces the vector knowledge base, and the entire companion is ready to guide.
4.3 Project Features
(1) Ultra-low-cost plug-and-play migration for covering an entire library of authors
From knowledge base construction to running the reading companion, every component is deliberately lightweight. A master’s knowledge base is populated solely from their public-domain works, letters, and critical biographies, then formatted through a standard embedding pipeline into a local vector store. The frontend builds on SageRead, eliminating the need to redevelop EPUB rendering, column layout, or typographic logic. This approach of reusing a robust skeleton and only injecting new knowledge means that any author whose works are in the public domain can be equipped with a personalized reading companion in a matter of hours. This creates a reusable engineering paradigm for scaling to dozens of literary figures without escalating cost or complexity.
(2) An immersion ethic that balances suspense safety with emotional restraint
Respect for the reader’s autonomous discovery is a non-negotiable performance indicator. The dual-layer anti-spoiler design ensures that the companion never leaps ahead of the reader’s pace, safeguarding the natural unfolding of the plot. Emotionally, the proactive Bubble mechanism is engineered to be sparse, appearing only at carefully selected narrative peaks, and every annotation is explicitly marked by the authorial perspective that generated it, so the reader always retains critical distance. This ethic of non-intrusive, suspense-safe, and emotionally restrained companionship stands in sharp contrast to entertainment chatbots. It builds a foundation of academic-level trust, making the tool suitable for serious study without polluting the reading experience with either spoilers or excessive affective manipulation.
(3) Native dual-mode design for comparative literature classrooms and dedicated book lovers
The system directly serves two vital user scenarios. In a literature classroom, a student can highlight a single passage and instantly switch between Lu Xun’s perspective and Oscar Wilde’s perspective, comparing the radically different diagnoses they would offer and deriving a thesis for analytical writing from that direct collision of worldviews. For the devoted fan exploring an author’s entire oeuvre, the character companion mode becomes a tool for deep character study, allowing the reader to interrogate a character’s hidden motives and then receive an inner monologue bubble that surfaces the subtext the original narrative only implies. The combination of meta-cognitive master annotations and character introspection turns the companion into an intellectual instrument that supports both structured pedagogical inquiry and the solitary pleasure of profound literary immersion.
Feasibility analysis of technical programmes
Development foundation
The system rests on two infrastructure decisions that define the resource envelope for all subsequent components.
Frontend skeleton. The open-source SageRead project (github.com/xincmm/sageread) provides a TypeScript-based three-column reading interface with built-in EPUB parsing, virtual DOM rendering optimized for long-form text, and a right-panel AI assistant slot. Its codebase handles the full complexity of e-reading layout including responsive column sizing, smooth scroll position tracking, and annotation sidebar management, none of which the team needs to rebuild. Three targeted extensions are grafted onto this skeleton: a floating Bubble annotation layer anchored to user-selected text via the @floating-ui/react positioning library, which handles viewport-aware placement and collision avoidance; a custom RAG retrieval backend that replaces the default AI connection and routes every annotation request through the project's knowledge pipeline; and a real-time reading-progress stream that continuously captures the reader's chapter index and scroll offset and injects this state into every inference request so that all AI responses are positionally aware. Building on a production-grade open-source base compresses the frontend delivery timeline significantly and allows the team's engineering effort to concentrate on the AI-layer components that constitute the project's primary contribution.
LLM inference. All base language model inference is handled through commercial APIs, with GPT-4o and DeepSeek-V3 as primary candidates, selected for their strong instruction-following capacity, long-context reliability, and well-documented API stability. This decision is driven by a hard hardware constraint: even parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods such as QLoRA require a minimum of 16 to 24 gigabytes of GPU VRAM for a 7-billion-parameter base model, which substantially exceeds the team's local ceiling of 8 gigabytes on an NVIDIA RTX 4060. API-based deployment removes GPU scheduling overhead, eliminates quantization and serving complexity, and scales gracefully under a pay-per-use pricing model sustainable at course-prototype query volumes. Current frontier models respond reliably to long and structurally complex system prompts, making prompt-level persona simulation viable without weight-level modification. The one exception is the MEGa long-term memory module, which requires LoRA adapter training and will be executed on a rented cloud GPU instance such as AutoDL, with the resulting adapter weights served alongside the API inference pipeline at runtime.
Retrieval-Augmented Generation Pipeline
Figure 11. The RAG architecture (Lewis et al., NeurIPS 2020), illustrating the dense retrieval component and the sequence-to-sequence generator.
Both core features of the system rely on Retrieval-Augmented Generation to ground model outputs in verified source material and suppress hallucination. The RAG paradigm augments a generative language model with a dense retrieval component: at inference time, a query is issued against an external vector store, the most semantically relevant passages are retrieved and prepended to the model's context window before generation begins. This architecture decouples factual knowledge from model parameters, allows the knowledge base to be updated or swapped without any retraining, and ensures that every generated annotation is traceable to a real, retrievable source rather than unconstrained model generation.
The ingestion pipeline is shared across both knowledge base types. Source documents are split into paragraph-level chunks preserving sentence boundaries, embedded with a lightweight multilingual embedding model such as BGE-M3 or text-embedding-3-small, and stored in a local vector database. The team has evaluated ChromaDB and Qdrant as candidate stores; both support the metadata pre-filtering required by the anti-spoiler mechanism and can run as embedded databases without a separate server process, keeping the deployment architecture simple.
For the Lead Reader feature, a dedicated per-author knowledge base is constructed from four categories of source material: the author's published literary and critical works, transcripts of public interviews and lectures, personal correspondence and diary entries available in the public domain, and scholarly critical biographies. Each author's corpus is kept in a separate vector store so that a query issued under one Lead Reader's perspective never surfaces materials from another, preserving the integrity of each persona. For the Character Companion feature, the knowledge base is the book text itself, segmented with positional metadata attached to every chunk.
At inference time, a composite retrieval query combining the highlighted text with several sentences of surrounding context is issued against the relevant store. The top-k retrieved chunks are ranked by cosine similarity, concatenated in source order, and injected into the model's context window as the factual grounding layer, ensuring that every Lead Reader comment and character response is anchored in real materials.
Dataset Construction and Evaluation Baseline
Given that the Muse Reading system heavily relies on Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and complex Prompt Engineering rather than full-model fine-tuning, the feasibility of the project hinges on the construction of highly structured knowledge bases and rigorous evaluation datasets. The dataset construction is divided into three core dimensions:
Structured RAG Corpus Construction and Metadata Engineering
For Literary Master Agents, we will construct distinct knowledge pools using public-domain works, critical biographies, and historical letters. For Book Character Agents, the raw EPUB/TXT files will be automatically parsed using a semantic, paragraph-level chunking strategy to preserve narrative context, avoiding the pitfalls of naive fixed-token splitting. Crucially, we will develop a robust automated data pipeline to inject a granular metadata schema into every text chunk. This schema will include strict locational vectors such as chapter index, section index, token offset, and entity tags like characters present and location. This precise locational metadata is the foundational requirement for the Narrative-Aware Gating anti-spoiler mechanism, enabling deterministic, database-level pre-filtering before vector similarity search even occurs.
Emotional Salience and Proactive Bubble Trigger Dataset
To support the Proactive Bubble trigger, we will construct a specialized dataset where narrative segments are quantitatively scored for emotional density. Instead of arbitrary triggers, we will establish a multi-dimensional scoring rubric evaluating interpersonal intensity (the presence of conflict or profound dialogue), psychological complexity (instances of character hesitation, realization, or deception), and symbolic density (the introduction of key thematic motifs). We will sample key chapters from classic literature to create a Golden Dataset of human-annotated segments. This golden baseline will be used to calibrate an LLM-assisted scoring pipeline, utilizing a human-in-the-loop verification process to generate a reliable, book-wide emotional topology index. This dataset ensures that AI interventions are triggered strictly by data-driven thresholds, making them feel well-timed and emotionally resonant rather than random or intrusive.
Red-Teaming Benchmarks for Persona and Anti-Spoiler Validation
To mathematically verify system safety and response quality, we will curate a custom evaluation benchmark divided into two adversarial testing sets. First, for the Spoiler-Trap Dataset (Red Teaming), we will generate a dataset of adversarial QA pairs designed to trick the agent into revealing future plot points, such as querying a Chapter 2 agent about a character's death in Chapter 10. The evaluation metric here is strict: the system must achieve a 0% Spoilage Leakage Rate to validate the dual-layer data-gate and prompt-guard anti-spoiler mechanism. Second, for the Persona Fidelity Benchmark, to validate the skill distillation prompts, we will construct a dataset of character-specific and author-specific interpretive queries. Outputs will be evaluated against baseline LLM responses using an LLM-as-a-judge framework, scoring the generation on analytical depth, logical structure (such as Lu Xun's dissection of social pathology), and stylistic adherence (such as Eileen Chang's clinical register).
Persona Simulation and Long-Term Memory
Prompt-level skill distillation for Lead Readers. A naive approach to persona simulation produces outputs that capture surface stylistic traits such as vocabulary register and sentence rhythm but miss the analytical character that makes a given author's perspective genuinely distinctive. The technique this system employs goes further, performing a zero-training form of skill distillation: the system prompt encodes not just an author's voice but the step-by-step reasoning pattern through which they characteristically construct an insight, making their cognitive approach explicit as a structured inference scaffold the model follows at generation time.
For Lu Xun, the scaffold proceeds in three stages: identify a mundane social detail the passage presents as unremarkable; expose the deep-rooted cultural pathology or collective psychological weakness it symptomizes; then connect it to a recurring historical pattern that reveals the condition as systemic. For Oscar Wilde, the scaffold operates through inversion: surface the conventional moral framing a mainstream reader would apply; construct a paradox that exposes the hidden philistinism or self-flattery concealed within that convention; resolve it into an aesthetically polished aphorism. For Eileen Chang, the scaffold centers on emotional ambivalence: locate the gap between what a character says and what the surrounding descriptive detail implies they feel; name the specific variety of modern urban loneliness or self-protective performance that fills the gap; frame it in Chang's characteristically cool, clinical register that refuses both sentimentality and condemnation. These scaffolds are crafted through close reading of each author's critical writings and encoded as explicit procedural instructions in the system prompt, which is re-injected at every inference call to prevent persona drift across multi-turn conversations.
Character agent design. Prior research has validated that large language models can simulate persona-consistent decisions with high fidelity when generation is conditioned on structured character memory retrieved from source text. The CharMap framework demonstrated this by constructing a memory map from the original work that organizes a character's documented motivations, relationships, and speech patterns into a retrievable structure, enabling responses that remain consistent with the character's established profile across diverse query types. The character companion in this system follows the same retrieval-conditioned architecture. Each agent receives a system prompt establishing its psychological profile including goals, fears, and characteristic speech register, while the RAG pipeline supplies passage-level context from the book at the reader's current position. This supports two interaction modes: passive response to direct user queries including casual questions about plot details or minor characters; and proactive first-person inner-monologue Bubbles at narratively significant moments, surfacing subtext and unexpressed motivation that the original text implies but does not state.
Figure 12. The CharMap framework for character memory retrieval (Wang et al., 2024).
Long-term memory via MEGa. Session-stateless persona simulation means that every new reading session begins with a character agent that has no memory of prior interactions: it cannot recall a reader's previously expressed interpretation, cannot track how understanding of a character has evolved chapter by chapter, and cannot allow the reader-character relationship to develop continuity over time. The MEGa (Memory Embedded in Gated LLMs) framework addresses this by encoding individual memories into dedicated gated LoRA adapter modules. At inference time, a semantic-similarity-based global gating signal selectively activates the modules most relevant to the current context, integrating the appropriate memories into generation without loading all stored memories simultaneously. The practical result is that the character agent can recall a reader's expressed sympathy for a particular character, recognize their tendency toward analytical rather than emotional engagement, and use this accumulated context to shape responses in ways that feel genuinely relational. Because MEGa requires LoRA training, this component is handled on a rented cloud GPU instance, with training triggered incrementally after each session to encode its most significant interactions. The resulting adapter weights are lightweight and load efficiently alongside the API inference pipeline at runtime.
Figure 13. The MEGa memory injection architecture using gated LoRA modules (Gottesman et al., 2025).
The recognized limitation of this approach is that prompt-level skill distillation does not achieve the stylistic depth that full supervised fine-tuning on author-specific corpora could theoretically produce. This trade-off is accepted as appropriate for the current scope: RAG grounding ensures factual accuracy, structured reasoning scaffolds ensure analytical character, and MEGa ensures relational continuity. Together these three components address the most perceptible dimensions of persona quality from the reader's perspective without the dataset construction and training infrastructure that fine-tuning would require.
5.4 Anti-Spoiler Mechanism and Proactive Bubble Trigger
Data-layer filtering. At ingestion time, every chunk in the book-text knowledge base is tagged with a positional metadata record containing its chapter index, section index, and token offset. This metadata is stored as a scalar field alongside each chunk's embedding vector. When anti-spoiler mode is active, the retrieval query is augmented with a hard scalar pre-filter restricting the candidate set to chunks whose chapter index is less than or equal to the reader's current chapter, enforced as a database-level constraint before any similarity computation begins. This sequencing is critical: the filter is applied at the data layer rather than as a post-processing step, so future-chapter content is never scored, ranked, or passed to the model under any circumstances. Both ChromaDB and Qdrant implement this class of metadata pre-filter natively, requiring no custom retrieval infrastructure. The guarantee is deterministic: regardless of how semantically similar a future-chapter passage might be to the current query, it cannot enter the model's context window through the retrieval path.
Prompt-layer constraint. A complementary behavioral constraint at the prompt level addresses the residual risk that the base model's parametric memorization of widely-discussed texts could allow spoiler information to surface even when the retrieval path is cleanly filtered. The character agent's system prompt includes an explicit high-priority instruction stating that the character has no awareness of narrative events beyond the reader's current chapter and must not draw on any external knowledge about the plot that was not supplied through the retrieval context. This constraint cannot provide the same mathematical certainty as the data-layer filter, but it substantially reduces the risk of leakage through model reasoning and closes the gap left by the hard filter. The combination of a deterministic data gate and a prompt-level behavioral guard constitutes a defense-in-depth architecture the project terms Narrative-Aware Gating. The reader's current chapter position, maintained in the real-time progress stream from the SageRead frontend, serves as the single source of truth for both the retrieval filter threshold and the prompt-level boundary instruction, ensuring the two layers remain synchronized at all times.
Proactive Bubble trigger. To allow characters to surface inner thoughts at contextually resonant moments without requiring explicit user queries, a lightweight orchestration algorithm monitors the reader's positional state against a pre-computed map of narrative emotional peaks. During knowledge base construction, each chunk is scored for emotional salience by prompting the base LLM to assess it on dimensions including interpersonal intensity, psychological complexity, symbolic density, and narrative consequence. The resulting salience score is stored as an additional metadata field, producing a saliency index covering the entire book. At runtime, when the reader pauses at a high-salience passage beyond a configurable dwell-time threshold, typically fifteen to thirty seconds, the orchestration layer triggers the character agent to generate a proactive Bubble. The annotation is constrained to a short first-person inner monologue expressing what the character is experiencing or concealing at that precise moment, grounded in the RAG context available up to the reader's current chapter, and held deliberately brief to avoid disrupting reading flow. The governing design principle is strict restraint: the trigger threshold and salience scoring are calibrated to produce interventions that are rare and well-timed, so that the reader experiences not a system generating pop-ups but a character confiding in them at exactly the moment such a confidence feels most natural.
