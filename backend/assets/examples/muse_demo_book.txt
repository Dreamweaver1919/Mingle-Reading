Chapter 1

Lin opened the old notebook because he wanted a cleaner reason to stay in the library than simple loneliness.

The room was quiet except for the scrape of chairs and the nervous rhythm of rain against the glass.

When Aya laughed at his careful notes, the sentence stung less than the ease with which she seemed to read through him.

Chapter 2

Aya returned the next afternoon with a page of corrections and a question: was he reading for knowledge, or for permission to feel something larger?

Lin pretended to disagree, but he kept her margin notes folded in his coat pocket as if they were a private map.

At dusk they argued about whether memory protects a person or merely slows down the next mistake.

Chapter 3

By the time the lights dimmed, the notebook had become less a record of facts than a ledger of unfinished courage.

Lin finally understood that the passages he copied most carefully were the ones that allowed him to delay a decision.

Aya did not answer directly; she only said that every reader eventually meets the paragraph that refuses to remain a paragraph.
